This looks like it was made in 1999 on purpose.

Websites used to be good, but I think they are all very bad now!

The internet used to feel small and personal. There was less polish, and the bones of every website were almost painfully visible. There's a charm to that, I think. It seemed more human. Like every person was given the same box of LEGOs and they arranged those shared blocks in different ways that communicated something about them.

Rather than try to hide my LEGO bricks, I wanted to emphasize them.

The guiding principle of the modern web is a focus on what the algorithm demands, not what either the creator or the visitors want. And the demands of the algorithm have created a sameness across the web. Creating websites that appeal to the broadest possible audience makes sense in a lot of instances.

But I wanted to make a website that pointedly ignores the algorithimic demands, and instead harkens back to an era before the algorithm defined the way we present to the world. An era when the web felt more intimate. And maybe part of that intimacy comes from how evident the bones of the website once were. The modern polished web looks sharp and is algorithmically superior, but it does so by obfuscating as much as possible the fact that there is a human behind these pages.

What's the point of this?

Primarily for me to have fun. The web is a delightful and magical place! Our websites should reflect our joys.

And an era where websites were just the Dancing Baby GIF from Ally McBeal and you didn't have to worry about whether your visit to the site was facilitating genocide in Myanmar or eroding democracy was simply a more joyous time to be online. And what are we even doing here if we're not enjoying ourselves?
